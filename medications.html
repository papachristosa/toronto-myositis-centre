Medications in myositis.

Clear, plain-language guides to the medicines used at the Centre, grouped by the job they do: what each one is, how it helps, its risks, and how you are kept safe. Every guide has a print button so you can take it home.

Understanding myositis →
!

This guide is for learning, not medical advice. Myositis care is different for everyone, and many of these medicines are used based on specialist experience rather than a formal Canadian myositis approval. Never start, stop, or change a medicine without your own doctor. Your doses are decided with your care team at the Centre.

Corticosteroids

Fast, strong anti-inflammatories, usually the first treatment while slower medicines take effect.

Prednisone

→
Corticosteroid

The fast, strong anti-inflammatory usually started first.
Steroid-sparing immune-suppressing medicines

Slower medicines that control the disease and let the steroid dose come down.

Methotrexate

→
DMARD

The most used steroid-sparing medicine, taken once a week.

Azathioprine

→
Immune-suppressing

A steroid-sparing option that is safe in pregnancy.

Mycophenolate

→
Immune-suppressing

A steroid-sparing medicine for muscle, skin, and lungs.

Hydroxychloroquine

→
Antimalarial / DMARD

A gentle long-term medicine, good for skin disease.
Immune therapies & targeted medicines

Antibody and targeted treatments, often for disease that needs more than first-line medicines.

IVIG

→
Immunoglobulin

An infusion with strong evidence in dermatomyositis.

Rituximab

→
B-cell therapy

A targeted antibody for disease that needs more.

Upadacitinib

→
JAK inhibitor

A once-daily targeted pill.

Tofacitinib

→
JAK inhibitor

A targeted pill, used like other JAK inhibitors.
For lung involvement (ILD)

Used when myositis affects the lungs. Mycophenolate, azathioprine, rituximab, and IVIG are also used here.

Tacrolimus

→
Calcineurin inhibitor

A steroid-sparing option, often used for the lungs.

Cyclophosphamide

→
Strong immune-suppressing

A strong, time-limited option for severe disease.

Nintedanib

→
Anti-scarring

Slows lung scarring, added to immune treatment.
For Raynaud's & finger ulcers

When cold turns fingers white and blue, or fingertips develop sores. A daily aspirin is sometimes added.

Calcium channel blockers

→
For Raynaud's

The usual first medicine to ease Raynaud's.

PDE5 inhibitors

→
Raynaud's / ulcers

Improve blood flow for severe Raynaud’s or ulcers.

Topical nitroglycerin

→
Applied to skin

A cream near a finger to improve local blood flow.

Bosentan

→
Prevents finger ulcers

Reduces new finger ulcers; needs monthly tests.

Iloprost

→
Infusion, severe cases

A drip for a severe or threatened finger.
For bone protection

Because long-term steroids weaken bone, these protect against fractures.

Calcium & vitamin D

→
Supportive

The base layer of bone protection on steroids.

Bisphosphonates

→
Bone protection

Tablets or a yearly drip to protect bone from steroids.

Denosumab

→
Bone protection

A twice-yearly injection to protect bone.
